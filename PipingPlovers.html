Piping Plover

A Threatened Shorebird of Canada

Overview and ID info

Piping plovers, Charadrius melodus, breed along the Eastern ocean shores and along lakeshores and rivers in the northern Great Plains and Great Lakes. They nest above the high water line in sandy areas with sparse vegetaion including marshes, ocean shores, bays, spoil islands, reservoirs, alkali lakes, and rivers. In the winter, they forage on coastal beaches, sandflats, and mudflats that are exposed during low tide.

These plover nest in areas with loose sand above the high tide line. Male Piping Plovers scrap away sand, gravel, and shells with their feet to make a small depression. They make several small depressions or scrapes in the sand within their territory typically near small climps of grass away from the water's edge, often near Least and Common Terns.

Nest scrapes or depressions take only a day to build but a pair can take 5-10 days to decide on which scrape to use. Once they choose, they line the scrape with small pebbles or bits of shell. Each depression is about 4 inches wide and 0.5 inches deep. Eggs are creamy with fine brownish black splotches.

A Threatened Species

Piping Plovers are extremely sensitive to disturbances on the beaches where it nests. As a result, it is not uncommon for adults to abandon viable nests. These birds are rare, with a global breeding population of just 8,400 individuals. Along the Atlantic coast they are listed as federally threatened. The Nature Conservancy of Canada state that there are fewer than 40 breeding pairs in Nova Scotia; a decline of more than 25 per cent since 2001. Piping Plovers breed on less than 30 beaches in this province, and the goal of conservation programs is to bring the number of breeding pairs up to 60.

Non-traditional habitats, such as parking lots, dredge spoils, or site with steep embankments are not formally identified as critical habitats. Conservation focus on protecting nests and nesting habitats by placing exclosures around nests to reduce the risk of predation or trampling. Effort has been made on predation control, controlling human recreation near breeding sites, restoring breeding sites such as sandbars, and abandoned egg rearing.

How to Help

As the biggest threat to Piping Plovers throughout their range is the loss or degradation of habitat resulting from the recreational use of beaches, being mindful and respective of their habitats is the easiest way to help this endangered species. ATVs and beachcombers often destroy nest or prompt the parents to abandon otherwise healthy eggs. In addition, garbage left behind attracts predators such as the red fox, raccoon, ring-billed gull, and crow.

Off-leash dogs are a large threat to birds who reside on public beaches. Dogs threaten all birds, not just plovers, by chasing them away or, in the case of Piping Plovers, crushing their eggs. Between 2012 and 2015, only 5 per cent of dogs were on leash and the highest levels of off-leash were at Martinique (pictured below) and Glace Bay beach. Keeping dogs on leash and walking close to the shore where the sand is wet. Obey signs that denote area closures due to breeding.
